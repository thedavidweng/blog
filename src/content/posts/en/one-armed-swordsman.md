### Introduction

Fu describes in the chapter **The Shaw Brothers Diasporic Cinema** in *China Forever: The Shaw Brothers and Diasporic Cinema*:

> Relying on the film distribution channels established through years of cultivation in the Southeast Asian region, the film studio that started in Shanghai and moved to Hong Kong, Shaw Brothers gradually established a huge cultural network across Asia, forming a stable factory-style one-stop film system, producing films rich in Chinese cultural traditions and spirit that can resonate, serving Chinese communities around the world who need cultural identity, until Shaw Brothers adapted to the development of the times for innovation, and began to decline after ruling Hong Kong cinema for more than ten years.

This is undoubtedly an accurate summary of Shaw Brothers' decade-long development process. In the process of Shaw Brothers occupying the market, martial arts films played a significant role, and "One-Armed Swordsman" is a microcosm of them. The background of "One-Armed Swordsman" is set in the jianghu, that fictional China with a unique romanticism that blends ancient Chinese dynasties. And the chivalry of the protagonist Fang Gang in "One-Armed Swordsman" runs through the entire film. Chang Cheh made changes that adapted to the times in theme, absorbed Shaw Brothers' years of filming experience and budget-saving techniques in form, and in fact led the trend of similar films.

### Masculinity

"One-Armed Swordsman" and "Come Drink with Me", as the two pioneering works of Shaw Brothers martial arts films, inevitably require a comparison. One of the most obvious differences between Chang Cheh and Hu Jinquan lies in Masculinity. Chang Cheh's films are more in line with our current understanding of the so-called martial arts genre. Chivalrous courage, pleasure in revenge.

The masculinity in Chang Cheh's films starts first with male actors.

This seems a bit counterintuitive, because in the current global environment of feminist movements criticizing patriarchy, Chang Cheh's innovation back then was actually shifting from a center on actresses to a center on actors. But carefully recalling the names of big stars that can be remembered from that era, Lin Dai, Luodi, and You Min were indeed all actresses.

Looking at Chang Cheh's films again, Wang Yu, Di Long, Jiang Dawei, Luo Lie, Fu Sheng, especially the period of Di Long and Jiang Dawei, chivalry and brotherhood completely overshadowed romantic plots, leading to CP fans of these two appearing back then, indirectly contributing to the creation of "Farewell My Concubine". (The inspiration for the original novel of "Farewell My Concubine" came from Li Bihua, and the name of the protagonist Duan Xiaolou also originated from Jiang Dawei's role in "Vengeance", Guan Xiaolou.)

It's quite interesting how it went full circle from politically incorrect back to politically correct.

### Aestheticization of Violence

When mentioning the aestheticization of violence, one thinks of Quentin Tarantino. The fact that Tarantino is a Hong Kong cinema fan is no longer a secret. The homage to Chang Cheh and other Shaw Brothers filmmakers in his debut film "Kill Bill" already started from the huge SB Logo (Shaw Brothers Studio) in the opening of "Kill Bill". The source of Tarantino's style is greatly influenced by Chang Cheh and his disciple John Woo.

Regardless of how weapons change in Chang Cheh and John Woo's films, whether knives or guns, they must be accompanied by scenes of skin torn and flesh exposed, intestines coming out. But different from B-movies that simply spray artificial blood plasma, the beauty in the aestheticization of violence is reflected in the proper form of poses, the shaping of emotion and atmosphere, and even the coolness of the protagonist. In "One-Armed Swordsman", Fang Gang asks for wine in the encirclement as if no one else is around. In "Vengeance", Guan Xiaolou walks towards the enemy in white clothes stained with blood.

So there is beauty in Chang Cheh's films, but it's different from the beauty in Hu Jinquan's films. The two directors have different focuses. If the martial arts fights in Hu Jinquan's films are like Kamen Rider mainstream works, then the martial arts fights in Chang Cheh's films are like Kamen Rider Amazons. In terms of purity, the latter might be better, but one cannot compare this way. Chang Cheh later walked further and further on the road to improving purity, until he turned martial arts films into cult films.

Ethan de Seife has another summary of Chang Cheh's films. He believes that there is a commonality in the narrative structure of Chang Cheh's films. In his words, "Brutal Mathematics", different plots are closely connected by numbers, making the stories of his films highly modular, which can be moved and exchanged, and can also freely control the length. The formation of this style is also inseparable from the Shaw Brothers factory system mentioned earlier. Understandable from an economic perspective, but also buries hidden dangers for Shaw Brothers to produce a large number of homogenized films at the artistic level.

The script of "One-Armed Swordsman" was written by Ni Kuang, the author of the Wuxia series and Yuan Zhenxia series. The setting basically copies the protagonist in Jin Yong's "The Return of the Condor Heroes" as per his good friend's work. According to Ni Kuang, the script was also substantially modified by Chang Cheh.
